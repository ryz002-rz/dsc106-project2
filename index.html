DSC 106 · Project 2 Persuasive / Deceptive Visualization

Two Charts.
One Dataset.
Two believable stories.

The same World Bank climate numbers can support very different conclusions. This page walks through how each narrative is constructed, what feels fair, and where persuasive framing can quietly become misleading.

Read this like a guided tour, not a verdict: each chart includes the design decisions behind it, scored from −2 (deceptive) to +2 (earnest), so you can see both the argument and the craft.

Group: Keith Gong (x4gong@ucsd.edu) · Roxanne Wang (rox005@ucsd.edu) · Ryan Zhang (ryz002@ucsd.edu) Course: DSC 106 — Spring 2026 Dataset: World Bank Climate Change Indicators (1960–2018)
The proposition
“China’s economic rise has made meaningful climate progress impossible.”

We built two pages from one dataset: one designed to argue the proposition is true, one designed to argue the opposite. Each visualization below is followed by the design choices that made it persuasive — scored on a diverging scale from −2 (fully deceptive) to +2 (fully earnest).

Scale: −2 deceptive −1 0 neutral +1 +2 earnest
1 Page 1 · For the proposition

China’s economic rise has made climate progress impossible.

This version is intentionally urgent. Reds dominate, values are absolute rather than normalized, and the visual tone nudges the reader toward a single-cause explanation.

Visualization 1

China Erased Every Gain the West Ever Made

The change in annual CO2 emissions from a 1990 baseline. China’s curve adds 8.1 billion tonnes; the combined West (US, Germany, UK, France, Canada, Japan) is essentially flat — barely −0.2 billion tonnes after thirty years.

  • −1.5 1990 baseline conveniently chosen

    Anchoring the y-axis at 1990 starts the clock just before China’s reform-era acceleration, maximizing visible divergence. Picking 1980, 2005, or a rolling baseline would produce a softer curve. We considered showing multiple baselines but chose the most dramatic.

  • −1.5 Six countries collapsed into “the West”

    Aggregating six economies into one line averages out high performers (UK, Germany) with laggards (Canada). Shown individually, several Western countries did meaningfully reduce. The aggregate flattens that variance and makes the West look uniformly stagnant against a single rising China.

  • −2 Absolute emissions, no per-capita normalization

    We show raw tonnes — a population term hidden in plain sight. China has roughly 4× the population of the US, so its absolute total is huge by construction. Reframing on per-capita would entirely flip the visual hierarchy. We picked the framing that makes scale read as guilt.

  • −1 Saturated reds and dark page palette

    Crimson connotes alarm, blood, danger; the dark background tightens the emotional register. The data could be plotted in any color, but red on near-black primes the reader to feel a problem before reading the axis.

  • +1 Source line and complete units stay visible

    Despite the slanted framing, the y-axis is correctly scaled, includes units (billion metric tonnes), and the source citation appears under the chart. A skeptical reader who looks past the headline can still recover the raw data.

Visualization 2

While Europe Invests in Clean Energy, China’s Renewables Share Has Plummeted

China’s renewable share of total final consumption fell from 34% (1990) to 13% (2018). Germany rose from 2% to 16% over the same window. Framed: China is moving the wrong way.

  • −2 Indicator definition silently exploited

    The World Bank’s “renewable energy consumption” figure includes traditional biomass — firewood, crop waste, and dung used for cooking and heating. China’s 1990 share of 34% is overwhelmingly rural biomass combustion, not solar or wind. Modernization replaced biomass with coal-fired electricity, so the line falls. We never explain that, and the title invites the reader to read the drop as a reversal of climate policy.

  • −1.5 Slanted title and annotations

    “Plummeted,” “headed backwards,” and “genuine transition” (next to Germany) are explicit value judgments laid on top of a neutral line chart. The annotations contrast Germany’s gain with China’s loss using language taken from policy debate, not from data.

  • −1 Comparison set chosen to flatter Germany

    Germany and the UK are renewable-energy leaders in Europe. Plotting against Poland, the US, or Japan would tell a different story (Poland is also coal-heavy and only modestly improved). We picked the strongest contrast available.

  • 0 Continuous line markers and axes

    The encoding itself — ordered, continuous lines, an axis from 0 to 42% — is conventional and not deceptive. The deception lives in the framing, not the geometry. We considered breaking the y-axis to amplify the gap but chose not to, since a misuse of axes would be too easily detected.

  • +1 Multi-country comparison provides context

    Showing three countries (rather than China alone) gives the reader a frame of reference. A China-only line would be more emotionally clean but feels cherry-picked; the comparison group adds a layer of apparent objectivity that a careful reader can also use to question the framing.

2 Page 2 · Against the proposition

The West built this crisis. China is just catching up.

This version is intentionally measured. A muted palette and structural chart choices (per-capita and cumulative views) make the argument feel calmer and more objective.

Visualization 3

An American Pollutes Twice as Much as a Chinese Person — Every Single Year

Per-capita CO2 in 2018: the US sits at 15.2t, Canada and Australia near 15.5t, China at 7.4t, India at 1.8t. The framing relocates moral accounting from the nation to the individual.

  • −1.5 Per-capita framing erases scale entirely

    Dividing by population transforms a country with billions of citizens into a comparable individual. This is mathematically valid but hides the fact that a low per-capita figure multiplied by 1.4 billion people is still a continent-sized emission total. The chart implicitly equates a Chinese citizen with an American one, then implies parity should mean rights.

  • −1.5 Comparison set tilted toward the case

    We picked seven countries that maximize contrast: US, Canada, Australia (high per-capita), India (very low), and three modest emitters. We omitted Qatar, Bahrain, Trinidad — tiny petro-states with extreme per-capita figures — because their inclusion would distract from the China-vs-US comparison we want to anchor. This isn’t a falsification, but it is a curated frame.

  • −1 Per-capita framing also elides export emissions

    A meaningful share of China’s emissions are produced making goods consumed in the West. Per-capita assigns those tonnes to Chinese citizens, even when the carbon ultimately serves an American shopper. The chart never mentions this, because acknowledging it would weaken the moral inversion we are setting up.

  • +1.5 Sorted, conventional bar geometry

    Horizontal bars sorted by value are the canonical encoding for ranked quantities; the reader can compare lengths at a glance. No truncation, no broken axes, all values labeled. The chart hides nothing in its geometry.

  • +2 Per-capita normalization is a real defensible metric

    Despite the slant, per-capita CO2 is a normatively meaningful indicator: the atmosphere is a shared global resource and its capacity to absorb carbon is finite, so equal individual access is a legitimate moral framing. The chart leans on a real argument, not a fabricated one.

Visualization 4

The West Built the CO2 Already in Our Atmosphere — China Is Just Catching Up

Cumulative emissions, 1960–2018: the US alone has emitted 279B tonnes; the combined West, 440B tonnes; China, 205B. Atmospheric CO2 is a stock, not a flow — the reframing reassigns responsibility toward the earlier industrializers.

  • −1 Start year of 1960 is selectively chosen

    Atmospheric CO2 from coal predates 1960 by more than a century. Starting in 1960 captures the bulk of post-war Western industrial output (good for the argument) but chops off the long 19th-century lead the West actually had — potentially understating, not overstating, the case. It’s also a year when Chinese output was minimal, ensuring the China bar starts low.

  • −1.5 “The West” aggregate stacked against single China

    The middle bar bundles six countries to compare against one. We could have shown each country individually (US, Germany, UK, France, Canada, Japan, China), but a single “West” bar towers more dramatically. Symmetry inversion of the same trick used on Page 1 to inflate China — here it inflates the West.

  • −0.5 Hatching as a visual handrail

    The diagonal hatching on the “West” bar reads as historical / archival. It frames the column as a “debt” that has been accumulating — an extra rhetorical layer beyond the bar height itself.

  • +2 Cumulative framing is physically correct for warming

    Radiative forcing responds to atmospheric concentration, which is the integral of past emissions, not the current-year flow. From a climate-physics standpoint this is the most defensible accounting of who has actually altered the atmosphere — even though it’s rhetorically chosen here.

  • +1.5 All values labeled; axes start at zero

    Each bar carries its absolute value; the y-axis starts at zero and uses a consistent unit (billion tonnes). The persuasive force comes from framing, not from any visual sleight-of-hand on the encoding itself.

Final reflection

Where persuasion ends and deception begins.

The hardest part of this assignment was discovering how often a defensible analytical choice and a deceptive one are the same choice. Per-capita normalization is an honest response to population differences and a way to dodge accountability for absolute scale; a 1990 baseline anchors the modern climate era and conveniently starts after China’s pre-reform decades; aggregating six countries into “the West” suppresses within-group variance whether you are using it to flatter Western reductions or to dramatize Western historical guilt. The visual encodings on every chart we built are conventional. The deception — where it exists — lives in framing decisions a reader cannot reverse-engineer from the chart alone: which years to show, which countries to compare, which denominator to divide by, what to call the title.

What surprised us is how loud the textual layer is. We expected color, axis tricks, and chart type to do most of the persuasive work. They didn’t. The single most powerful deceptive move on Page 1 is the indicator-definition trick in Visualization 2 — the fact that “renewable energy share” in the World Bank dataset includes traditional biomass, so a country modernizing out of rural poverty looks like it is reversing climate policy. No axis is broken, no scale is truncated; the deception is entirely in what we did not say. Conversely, the most persuasive earnest move on Page 2 is also definitional: choosing cumulative tonnes because warming responds to the integral of emissions, not to the current year. Same data, opposite stories, both built mostly out of word choices and metric choices.

Which side we lean toward

We lean toward Page 2. Per-capita and cumulative framings rest on physical and moral claims we find defensible: the atmosphere’s absorptive capacity is shared, and the warming we observe today is a stock built mostly by industrialized economies. That said, we resist calling Page 1 “just deceptive” — the global carbon budget is indifferent to historical fault once it is exhausted, and current Chinese annual output is a real collective-action problem. Both pages use real numbers.

So where would we draw the line? We came out of this thinking ethical visualization is less about what a chart looks like and more about what a chart asks the reader to assume. Encodings that lie on the geometry — truncated zero baselines, inconsistent axes, area scaled to the wrong dimension — are clearly out of bounds because they make the visual itself untrue. But framing choices — per-capita versus absolute, cumulative versus annual, this baseline year versus that one — only become deceptive when they are presented as the single true frame and the alternatives are hidden. Acceptable persuasion, by our reading, is honest about its own framing: it stays defensible after the alternatives are spelled out. The visualizations on this page mostly aren’t. Building them deliberately deceptively is exactly what made the line we are trying to draw legible.
